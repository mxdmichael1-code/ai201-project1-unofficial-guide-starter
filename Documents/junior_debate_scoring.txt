JUNIOR_DEBATE_SCORING
Document type: Judge training material
Format: WSDA Junior Extemporaneous Debate
Primary users: Junior debate judges
Purpose: Explain Junior Debate judging criteria, score range, point calculation, ranking, and ballot expectations.

SECTION 1: SCORING OVERVIEW

Junior Debate is scored from 18 to 30 points.

18 points is the base score.

Judges should add points above 18 based on the debater's performance in the six evaluation criteria.

The six evaluation criteria are:
1. Analysis.
2. Reasoning.
3. Rebuttal.
4. Cross Examination.
5. Delivery.
6. Overall Impact.

Judges should score based on what the debater does in the round.

SECTION 2: HOW TO ADD POINTS FROM 18

Judges should begin from the base score of 18.

For each of the six evaluation criteria, the judge may add points according to performance.

If the debater's performance in a criterion is insufficient, add 0 points for that criterion.

If the debater reaches an average level in a criterion, add 0.5 points for that criterion.

If the debater is above average in a criterion, add 1 point for that criterion.

If the debater is strong in a criterion, add 1.5 points for that criterion.

If the debater is very strong and clearly stands out in a criterion, add 2 points for that criterion.

Because there are six criteria and each criterion can add up to 2 points, the maximum score is 30.

The scoring formula is:
18 base points plus added points from the six criteria equals the final score.

SECTION 3: CRITERION 1 - ANALYSIS

Analysis evaluates whether the student explains the main issue of the topic.

A strong debater should look at the issue from an objective angle.

A strong debater should present arguments that have some impact.

A strong debater should clearly explain their own arguments.

Weak analysis may rely only on personal preference or unsupported opinion.

For example, saying "I think cats are better than dogs because my father said so" is not persuasive analysis.

Judges should ask:
1. Did the debater identify the main issue?
2. Did the debater explain the issue clearly?
3. Did the argument have impact?
4. Did the debater avoid relying only on personal opinion?

SECTION 4: CRITERION 2 - REASONING

Reasoning evaluates the student's logical support.

Judges should consider whether the debater's explanation is logical.

Judges should consider whether the speech connects ideas clearly.

Judges should consider whether claims are supported by reasons.

Strong reasoning makes it clear why the judge should believe the argument.

Weak reasoning may jump from a claim to a conclusion without enough explanation.

SECTION 5: CRITERION 3 - REBUTTAL

Rebuttal evaluates whether the student effectively responds to the opponent's arguments.

A strong rebuttal shows the judge why the opponent's argument is flawed.

A debater may attack the opponent's assumption, logic, example, impact, or conclusion.

A rebuttal should be specific.

A weak rebuttal may ignore the opponent's main argument or only repeat the debater's own case.

Judges should ask:
1. Did the debater answer the opponent's main point?
2. Was the response specific?
3. Did the response explain why the opponent's argument is weak?
4. Did the debater avoid missing important arguments?

SECTION 6: CRITERION 4 - CROSS EXAMINATION

Cross Examination evaluates the interaction between the two debaters during questioning.

Judges should consider whether the debater asks and answers clearly, concisely, and politely.

A strong question should help clarify the debate or challenge the opponent's reasoning.

A strong answer should respond to the question and defend the debater's position.

A debater should actively explain their own reasoning and effectively challenge the opponent's argument.

A weak cross examination may be rude, unclear, evasive, too long, or unrelated to the main issues.

Judges should also consider whether the debater keeps the correct role as questioner or answerer.

SECTION 7: CRITERION 5 - DELIVERY

Delivery evaluates the debater's speaking style.

Judges should consider whether the debater's expression is clear, accurate, efficient, and not unnecessarily slow or repetitive.

Strong delivery helps the judge understand the argument.

Weak delivery may make the debate hard to follow even if the idea itself is reasonable.

Delivery includes clarity, organization, pace, volume, fluency, confidence, and time control.

Judges should not reward style alone if the argument has weak analysis or reasoning.

SECTION 8: CRITERION 6 - OVERALL IMPACT

Overall Impact evaluates the persuasive effect of the whole debate performance.

Judges should consider whether the debater's performance is convincing and influential overall.

A strong overall impact means the debater gives the judge a clear reason to prefer their side.

Overall impact may come from strong analysis, clear reasoning, effective rebuttal, good cross examination, and persuasive delivery working together.

A weak overall impact means the performance does not leave a clear reason to vote for that side.

SECTION 9: EVIDENCE AND SCORING

Use of evidence is allowed when the tournament setting permits it.

However, evidence is not the key evaluating factor for Junior Debate.

Because Junior Debate uses limited preparation time, judges should not decide the round mainly based on who has more evidence.

Judges should focus more on explanation, logic, interaction, rebuttal, delivery, and overall persuasiveness.

A debater who uses evidence should still explain what the evidence proves and why it matters.

A debater who does not use evidence can still score well if their analysis, reasoning, rebuttal, cross examination, delivery, and overall impact are strong.

SECTION 10: RANKING AND DECISION

Judges should decide which debater performed better in the round.

Judges should use the six evaluation criteria to guide both scoring and ranking.

The higher-scoring debater should normally be ranked ahead.

Judges should avoid score-rank inconsistency (no lower point wins).

Judges should not decide based only on speaking confidence, personal agreement with the topic, or evidence quantity.

Judges should not introduce their own arguments or outside knowledge into the decision.

SECTION 11: BALLOT EXPECTATIONS

The Junior Debate ballot should include the topic, competitor names, competitor numbers, score, rank, and comments according to the tournament system.

Judges should record competitor information carefully.

Judges should make sure the score reflects the six criteria.

Judges should make sure the final score is calculated from 18 plus added points for the criteria.

Comments should explain the main strengths and weaknesses of the debater's performance.

Useful comments may mention analysis, reasoning, rebuttal, cross examination, delivery, or overall impact.

SECTION 12: QUICK REFERENCE

Junior Debate scores range from 18 to 30.

18 is the base score.

Judges add points based on six evaluation criteria.

The six criteria are analysis, reasoning, rebuttal, cross examination, delivery, and overall impact.

For each criterion, insufficient performance adds 0 points.

Average performance adds 0.5 points.

Above-average performance adds 1 point.

Strong performance adds 1.5 points.

Very strong and outstanding performance adds 2 points.

Evidence use is allowed when permitted, but it is not the key deciding factor.

Judges should focus on analysis, reasoning, rebuttal, cross examination, delivery, and overall impact.

JUNIOR_SPEECH_SCORING
Document type: Judge training material
Format: WSDA Junior Speech
Primary users: Junior speech judges
Purpose: Explain Junior Speech scoring, ranking, feedback levels, and the ten judging criteria.

SECTION 1: SCORING AND RANKING OVERVIEW

Judges evaluate each speaker based on the speaker's overall performance.

Judges give each speaker a score and a rank within the group.

The score range for Junior Speech is 90 to 100 points.

A higher score should correspond to a higher ranking in the group.

Judges should rank speakers according to their relative performance in the same group.

SECTION 2: FEEDBACK LEVELS

The ballot includes ten judging criteria.

For each criterion, judges may mark a feedback level.

The five feedback levels are:
1. Below Average.
2. Average.
3. Above Average.
4. Good.
5. Excellent.

The feedback levels do not directly determine the final score or rank.

A judge should use the feedback levels to help the speaker understand strengths and weaknesses.

The final score and rank should still be based on the speaker's overall performance.

SECTION 3: CRITERION 1 - ARTICULATION

Articulation evaluates the speaker's English pronunciation.

Judges should consider whether the speaker's pronunciation is clear and understandable.

Judges should consider whether the speaker uses correct pronunciation patterns.

Strong articulation helps the judge and audience understand the speech.

Weak articulation may make the speech difficult to follow.

SECTION 4: CRITERION 2 - EMPHASIS

Emphasis evaluates whether the speaker highlights important words, phrases, and sentences.

A speaker may use pauses, stronger volume, or vocal stress to show emphasis.

Good emphasis helps the audience identify important ideas.

Weak emphasis may make the speech sound flat or make key points difficult to notice.

SECTION 5: CRITERION 3 - SPEAKING RATE

Speaking rate evaluates whether the speaker's speed is appropriate.

A speaker should not speak so quickly that the judge cannot understand the main content.

A speaker should not speak so slowly that the speech has long pauses or loses energy.

A strong speaking rate is controlled, understandable, and appropriate for the speech content.

SECTION 6: CRITERION 4 - VOLUME

Volume evaluates whether the speaker controls voice level appropriately.

The speaker should be loud enough for the judge and audience to hear clearly.

The speaker should avoid speaking too softly.

The speaker should also avoid shouting or using volume in a way that feels uncontrolled.

Good volume supports clarity and audience engagement.

SECTION 7: CRITERION 5 - FACIAL EXPRESSION

Facial expression evaluates whether the speaker uses facial expression effectively.

This includes eye contact with the judge and audience.

The speaker should not look only at cue cards or the floor.

Good facial expression helps create connection with the audience.

Weak facial expression may make the speech feel less engaging.

SECTION 8: CRITERION 6 - HAND GESTURES

Hand gestures evaluate whether the speaker uses appropriate gestures and movements during the speech.

Gestures should support the meaning of the speech.

Gestures should not feel stiff, distracting, random, or overly artificial.

Speakers should avoid playing with clothing or making distracting repeated movements.

Good gestures help express ideas clearly and naturally.

SECTION 9: CRITERION 7 - BODY POSITION

Body position evaluates posture and stage position.

The speaker should show appropriate posture.

The speaker should avoid standing completely still in a way that weakens the presentation.

The speaker should also avoid walking around constantly in a distracting way.

Good body position supports confidence, control, and stage presence.

SECTION 10: CRITERION 8 - CREATIVITY OF CONTENT

Creativity of content evaluates whether the speech content is interesting, fresh, or imaginative.

The content does not have to be original.

A speaker may quote or use creative content from others or outside sources.

The judging focus is not whether the idea was originally created by the speaker.

The judging focus is how the speaker uses creative ideas in the speech.

Strong creativity makes the speech more interesting and engaging.

SECTION 11: CRITERION 9 - ORGANIZATION OF CONTENT

Organization of content evaluates whether the speech is logical and well structured.

The parts of the speech should connect to each other.

The audience should be able to follow the beginning, middle, and ending of the speech.

A well-organized speech makes the speaker's message easier to understand.

Weak organization may make the speech feel confusing or disconnected.

SECTION 12: CRITERION 10 - OVERALL IMPACT

Overall impact evaluates the total effect of the speech.

Judges should consider whether the speech is effective as a whole.

Overall impact includes content, delivery, clarity, organization, creativity, and audience connection.

A speech with strong overall impact leaves a clear impression on the judge and audience.

A speech with weak overall impact may be understandable but not especially effective.

SECTION 13: DELIVERY-FOCUSED CRITERIA

In WSDA Junior Speech, most criteria focus on speech delivery and presentation.

Among the ten criteria, seven mainly evaluate presentation form:
1. Articulation.
2. Emphasis.
3. Speaking Rate.
4. Volume.
5. Facial Expression.
6. Hand Gestures.
7. Body Position.

This means a large part of the judging standard focuses on whether the speaker can independently complete a speech and present it vividly and confidently to the audience.

The remaining criteria focus more on content creativity, content organization, and total speech effect.

SECTION 14: SCORE, RANK, AND COMMENTS

The ballot includes the competitor name, competitor number, round, speech time, speech topic, score, rank, and judging criteria.

Judges should make sure the competitor information is recorded correctly.

Judges should make sure the score and rank are consistent.

Judges should provide comments based on the speaker's actual performance.

Useful comments may mention pronunciation, emphasis, speed, volume, eye contact, gestures, posture, content creativity, organization, or overall impact.

Comments should be specific enough for the speaker to understand what went well and what can improve.

SECTION 15: QUICK REFERENCE

Junior Speech score range is 90 to 100 points.

Judges give both score and rank.

The ballot includes ten criteria.

Feedback levels are Below Average, Average, Above Average, Good, and Excellent.

Feedback levels do not directly determine rank or score.

The ten criteria are articulation, emphasis, speaking rate, volume, facial expression, hand gestures, body position, creativity of content, organization of content, and overall impact.

Seven of the ten criteria mainly evaluate delivery and presentation.

Creativity of content does not require the speech to be original.

Overall impact evaluates the total effectiveness of the speech.

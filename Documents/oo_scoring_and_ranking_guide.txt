ORIGINAL_ORATORY_SCORING_AND_RANKING_GUIDE
Document type: Judge training material
Format: Original Oratory (OO)
Primary users: Speech judges
Purpose: Explain Original Oratory scoring, ranking, advancement calculations, and the three judging criteria: importance, relevance, and originality.

SECTION 1: SCORING RESPONSIBILITY

Original Oratory is scored by judges.

Judges should score each speaker based on the performance in the assigned group.

Judges should also rank the speakers in the group according to their relative performance.

The score range is 90 to 100 points.

A higher score should correspond to a higher rank.

If two speakers receive the same score, the judge still needs to distinguish their ranking. Speakers may have the same score, but they should not have the same rank.

SECTION 2: GROUP RANKING

Judges rank speakers within the same group.

The strongest performance in the group should receive the highest placement, usually rank 1.

The next strongest performance should receive the next rank, and so on.

Judges should make sure that the score and rank are consistent. A speaker with a higher score should normally be ranked ahead of a speaker with a lower score.

Judges should avoid giving a very high score to a speaker while ranking that speaker low in the group unless the reason is clear.

If a speaker exceeds the maximum amount of time given (5 minutes 30 seconds for middle school division, 10 minutes 30 seconds for high school division or the combined division), then the speaker cannot be ranked first in that particular round.



SECTION 3: SCORING CRITERION 1 - IMPORTANCE

Importance asks whether the speaker chooses a topic that matters to individuals and society.

The topic should have significance beyond being personally interesting to the speaker.

The speech should build credibility and provide examples that support why the topic is important.

The speaker should use an appropriate speaking style to show the importance of the topic.

Judges should ask:
1. Is the topic meaningful/significant?
2. Does the speaker explain why the topic matters?
3. Is the thesis clearly established?
4. Does the delivery help the audience understand the importance of the topic?

A speech may be important because it addresses a social issue, explains a personal or community concern, warns about a risk, or helps the audience understand a meaningful idea.

SECTION 4: SCORING CRITERION 2 - RELEVANCE

Relevance asks whether the speaker connects the audience to the topic.

The speaker should help listeners understand how the topic affects individuals, communities, or society.

The speaker should use personal and distinctive expression to make the topic feel connected to real people.

The speech should not feel distant, abstract, or disconnected from the audience.

The speaker's rhetorical methods should be inclusive and accessible so that the audience can receive and understand the message.

Judges should ask:
1. Does the speaker connect the topic to the audience?
2. Does the speaker explain how the topic affects others or society?
3. Does the speaker use personal or distinctive expression?
4. Are the rhetorical methods inclusive and easy for the audience to follow?

A relevant speech makes the audience understand why the topic should matter to them or to people around them.

SECTION 5: SCORING CRITERION 3 - ORIGINALITY

Originality asks whether the speaker presents the topic in a personal, creative, or distinctive way.

The speaker should not simply repeat a common idea in a predictable way.

The examples or arguments should feel fresh, interesting, or engaging to the audience.

Originality can appear in the topic, structure, examples, language, humor, storytelling, perspective, or method of presentation.

Judges should ask:
1. Does the speech show a personal or creative approach?
2. Are the examples or arguments interesting or fresh?
3. Does the speaker present the topic in a way that attracts the audience?
4. Does the speech feel like the speaker's own work?

A speech can be original even if the topic itself is familiar, as long as the speaker handles the topic in a distinctive and effective way.

SECTION 6: HOW TO APPLY THE THREE CRITERIA TOGETHER

Judges should consider importance, relevance, and originality together.

A strong speech usually has a meaningful topic, a clear connection to the audience, and a distinctive way of presenting the idea.

A speech with an important topic may still rank lower if it is not relevant to the audience or lacks originality.

A speech with creative language may still rank lower if the topic does not seem meaningful or the message is unclear.

A speech that connects well with the audience may still rank lower if it does not show enough originality or depth.

Judges should evaluate the complete performance rather than scoring each criterion in isolation.

SECTION 7: BALLOT COMMENTS

The judge's comments should help the speaker understand the ranking and score.

Comments should be specific.

A useful comment may mention the topic's importance, the strength of audience connection, the originality of examples, or the clarity of delivery.

A weak comment is too general, such as "good job" or "nice speech," without explaining what was strong or what could improve.

Judges should connect feedback to the three scoring criteria when possible.

SECTION 8: QUICK REFERENCE

Original Oratory scores range from 90 to 100.

Higher scores should correspond to higher ranks.

Same scores still require different ranks if the ballot requires distinct placements.

Overall ranking first refers to the personal rank sum.

A lower personal rank sum is better.

If rank sums are tied, total personal score is used.

A higher personal score total is better.

Except for the final round, results may be cumulative across rounds.

Final round ranking does not accumulate previous round results.

Final round ranking is based only on final round judge scoring.

The three scoring criteria are importance, relevance, and originality.

Importance means the topic matters to individuals and society.

Relevance means the speaker connects the audience to the topic.

Originality means the speaker presents the topic in a personal, creative, or distinctive way.

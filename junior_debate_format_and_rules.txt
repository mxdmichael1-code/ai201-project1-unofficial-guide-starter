JUNIOR_DEBATE_FORMAT_AND_RULES
Document type: Judge training material
Format: WSDA Junior Extemporaneous Debate
Primary users: Junior debate judges
Purpose: Explain Junior Debate format, topic release, preparation procedure, speech order, timing, prep time, evidence use, side confirmation, and judge escalation rules.

SECTION 1: WHAT JUNIOR DEBATE IS

Junior Debate is an extemporaneous debate format.

Each round has one pro debater and one con debater.

The two debaters debate one resolution or topic.

The system randomly assigns each debater's side.

The pro debater supports the resolution.

The con debater opposes the resolution.

In every round, the pro debater always gives the first speech.

SECTION 2: TOPIC SELECTION

In each round and each flight, debaters will receive a different independent topic.

Debaters must prepare the assigned topic within the required preparation time.

The debate topic for each round will usually be announced in the judge WeChat group; if the judges are unsure about the topic, they should ask in the group to obtain clarification

To protect fairness, the extemporaneous topic should be released only 15 minutes before the official start of that round.

SECTION 3: PREPARATION GROUPS

After an extemporaneous topic is released, both pro and con debaters have 15 minutes to prepare.

During preparation, debaters may not receive outside assistance.

After preparation groups are assigned, the side generally should not be changed.

The round cannot be changed once confirmed unless tournament staff or the tabroom instructs otherwise.

SECTION 4: WHEN DEBATERS ENTER THE ROOM

There are two possible situations when debaters enter the competition room.

In the first situation, debaters have already completed their 15-minute preparation in a separate room or preparation area. If this is the case, the judge should confirm the debaters, confirm their sides, and start the debate right away.

In the second situation, debaters have not yet received the topic or are not aware of the topic. If this is the case, the judge should not start the debate immediately.

The judge should first confirm that both debaters are in the correct room.

The judge should confirm which debater is pro and which debater is con.

The judge should release the debate topic exactly as instructed in the WeChat group chat or official tournament communication.

The judge should make sure both debaters understand the topic and know which side they are debating before preparation begins.

The judge should start a 15-minute timer for preparation.

When the 15-minute preparation time is over, the judge should start the round.

SECTION 5: SIDE CONFIRMATION

Before every Junior Debate round, the judge should confirm each debater's side.

This side confirmation applies whether the debaters prepared in another room or will prepare inside the competition room.

The judge should confirm:
1. which debater is pro;
2. which debater is con;
3. that both debaters understand their assigned side;
4. that the topic is correct;
5. that the room and round match the tournament assignment.

Judges should be careful because side mistakes may affect fairness and tournament results.

SECTION 6: UNEXPECTED ISSUES BEFORE OR DURING THE ROUND

If an unexpected issue happens, the judge should report it to the tabroom and wait for further instruction.

Unexpected issues include:
1. a debater prepared the wrong side;
2. a debater does not know which side they are on after preparation time is over;
3. a debater is late;
4. the debaters appear to have different topics;
5. the judge is unsure whether the round assignment is correct;
6. a preparation group or room assignment appears incorrect;
7. there is disagreement about whether preparation time has already occurred.

Judges should not independently change the round, switch debater sides, restart preparation, or make a penalty decision unless instructed by the tabroom or tournament staff.

SECTION 7: OVERTIME RULE

Debaters must stop speaking immediately when their speech time ends.

Debaters may not continue speaking after time expires.

Judges should apply the time rule consistently to both sides.

Arguments made after time expires should normally not receive weight.

SECTION 8: MATERIAL AND INTERNET USE

Online competitions allow debaters to use computers and internet search to find materials.

However, debaters may not receive outside assistance in any form.

Outside assistance includes help from coaches, teachers, parents, other students, or anyone outside the round or preparation group.

If outside assistance is discovered, the competitor may be disqualified according to tournament rules. If the judge suspects such action, they should consult the tabroom for further assistance.

In offline competitions, during preparation after the topic is released and during the debate itself, debaters may not use paper materials, electronic devices, the internet, or external evidence lookup.

Because preparation time is limited, the use of evidence is not the key deciding factor in Junior Debate.

Evidence may be used if the format and tournament setting allow it, but judges should not treat evidence as the main evaluation factor.

Judges should focus more on analysis, reasoning, rebuttal, cross examination, delivery, and overall impact.

SECTION 9: ROUND LENGTH AND MAIN STAGES

Each Junior Debate round usually lasts about 15 to 20 minutes.

The round has four main stages:
1. constructive speeches;
2. cross examination;
3. rebuttal speeches;
4. final focus or summary speeches.

Each speech is usually 1 to 2 minutes long, depending on the specific stage.

Judges should follow the official timing table for the round.

SECTION 10: Procedure for junior debate

The round follows this order:

1. Pro Constructive: 2 minutes.
2. Cross Examination of the Pro by the Con: 1 minute.
3. Con Constructive: 2 minutes.
4. Cross Examination of the Con by the Pro: 1 minute.
5. Mandatory Prep Time: 1 minute.
6. Pro Rebuttal: 2 minutes.
7. Con Rebuttal: 2 minutes.
8. Mandatory Prep Time: 1 minute.
9. Pro Final Focus: 2 minutes.
10. Con Final Focus: 2 minutes.

The pro debater always starts with the first constructive speech.

The con debater asks questions after the pro constructive.

The pro debater asks questions after the con constructive.

Both debaters receive mandatory preparation time before rebuttals and before final focus speeches.

SECTION 11: CONSTRUCTIVE SPEECHES AND CROSS EXAMINATION

In the constructive speech, each debater presents their position.

The pro constructive presents a case or position in favor of the topic.

The con constructive presents a case or position against the topic.

After each constructive speech, the opposing debater asks questions.

During cross examination, the questioner should ask questions and the speaker being questioned should answer.

A debater should keep the correct role during cross examination.

The debater being questioned should not avoid answering, refuse to answer, or turn the cross examination into their own questioning period.

If a debater fails to keep the correct role during cross examination, the debater may lose points.

In extreme cases, serious refusal to participate properly in cross examination may cause the judge to vote against that side.

SECTION 12: REBUTTAL SPEECHES

In rebuttal speeches, debaters should respond to the opponent's main ideas.

A debater should identify the key problems in the opponent's arguments or reasoning.

A debater may show that the opponent's theory is flawed.

A debater may turn the opponent's reasoning against them.

A debater may present effective responses against the opponent's arguments.

A rebuttal should not only repeat the debater's own constructive speech.

SECTION 13: FINAL FOCUS OR SUMMARY SPEECHES

In final focus or summary speeches, debaters summarize the main points of disagreement.

Each final focus speech is 2 minutes.

Debaters should explain why they should win the debate.

Debaters should not introduce new arguments in this final stage.

The final speech should focus on the existing clash between the two sides.

SECTION 14: MANDATORY PREP TIME

There is 1 minute of mandatory prep time after the constructive and cross examination stage.

There is another 1 minute of mandatory prep time after the rebuttal stage.

This prep time must be used.

This prep time may not be divided into smaller parts.

Judges should time the mandatory prep periods and start the next speech when prep time is over.

SECTION 15: QUICK REFERENCE FOR JUDGES

Junior Debate is one pro debater versus one con debater.

Sides are randomly assigned by the system.

The pro debater always speaks first.

If debaters already prepared in a separate room, confirm sides and start the debate.

If debaters have not received the topic, release the topic as instructed, confirm sides, start 15 minutes of preparation, and then begin the round.

The round cannot be changed once confirmed unless tournament staff instructs otherwise.

Unexpected issues should be reported to the tabroom.

Debaters must stop speaking when time expires.

Online rounds allow computer and internet research, but no outside assistance.

Offline rounds do not allow paper materials, electronic devices, internet lookup, or evidence lookup during preparation or the debate.

Evidence use is allowed only when tournament setting permits it, but it is not the key deciding factor in Junior Debate.

Mandatory prep time is 1 minute before rebuttals and 1 minute before final focus.

Final focus should not include new arguments.

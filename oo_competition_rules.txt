ORIGINAL_ORATORY_COMPETITION_RULES
Document type: Judge training material
Format: Original Oratory (OO)
Primary users: Speech judges
Purpose: Explain Original Oratory competition rules on timing, manuscript use, props, generative AI, and academic integrity.

SECTION 1: OVERTIME RULE

In Original Oratory, the junior division speech time limit is 5 minutes.

The senior division speech time limit is 10 minutes.

All divisions have an additional 30-second grace period.

Speakers should keep their speech within the official time limit and the additional 30-second grace period.

If a speech exceeds the additional 30-second grace period, the competitor may continue their speech (the judge should not interrupt the speaker within reasonable overtime period) but can not be ranked first in the group.

Each round uses a timer, and the judge should time speakers' speech time (count-up timing) and record the time on the ballot after each speech.

There is no minimum speech time.

When junior and senior divisions are combined, all rounds use the longest 10-minute time limit.

A shorter speech should not be penalized only because it is shorter than 10 minutes.

Judges should evaluate whether the speech is effective, not simply whether it is long.

SECTION 2: MANUSCRIPT AND MEMORIZATION RULE

Original Oratory speeches are recommended to be delivered from memory.

A speech that is not delivered from memory may affect the judge's direct impression of the speaker.

Because memorization affects presentation quality, a non-memorized speech may affect the judge's score.

Judges should not treat manuscript use as an automatic disqualification but may slightly downgrade the speaker's performance and write it as feedback in the ballot.

Judges should consider how the use of notes or a manuscript affects delivery, eye contact, fluency, audience connection, and overall performance.

SECTION 3: PROP AND ASSISTIVE DEVICE RULE

Speakers may not use assistive equipment or tools during the speech.

Prohibited items include but are not limited to:
1. PowerPoint slides;
2. music;
3. video;
4. physical props;
5. other presentation tools or supporting devices.

Original Oratory should be evaluated based on the speaker's speech content and delivery, not on external presentation materials.

Judges should not reward a speaker for using prohibited props or presentation tools.

If a judge is unsure whether something counts as a prohibited tool, the judge should report the issue to tournament staff or the tabroom.

SECTION 4: GENERATIVE AI USE

In Original Oratory, students are prohibited from directly quoting text from generative AI sources.

Students are also prohibited from rewriting or paraphrasing text from generative AI sources as part of their speech.

Generative AI may not be used as a cited source.

One exception applies when a student gives a speech about artificial intelligence. In that situation, the student may quote AI-generated content to support a point about artificial intelligence.

Students may use generative AI to help find articles, ideas, or sources.

However, any quoted or paraphrased evidence must have an original source that can be provided if requested.

Judges should distinguish between using AI to locate sources and using AI as the source of speech text.

If a judge has a serious concern about AI misuse, the judge should report the concern to tournament staff or tabroom instead of directly accusing the student during the round.

SECTION 5: ACADEMIC INTEGRITY RULE

Academic misconduct means behavior by a competitor or coach that violates accepted academic standards or academic honesty during participation in WSDA Original Oratory.

Academic misconduct threatens the fairness of the tournament and the intellectual property of original works.

To protect academic integrity and tournament fairness, all Original Oratory competitors must cooperate with the committee if asked to submit their speech manuscripts for review.

The manuscript submitted by the competitor must match the content performed in the round.

A competitor should not submit a different version that does not reflect the actual speech used in competition.

Judges should not conduct their own investigation during the round unless instructed by tournament staff.

If a judge has a concern about plagiarism, AI misuse, manuscript mismatch, or other academic integrity issues, the judge should report it to tournament staff or tabroom.

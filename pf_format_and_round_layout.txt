PF_DEBATE_FORMAT_AND_ROUND_LAYOUT
Document type: Judge training material
Format: Public Forum Debate (PF)
Primary users: New and intermediate debate judges
Purpose: Explain the basic PF format, resolution system, coin flip, side selection, seating, full speech order, and preparation time rules.

SECTION 1: WHAT PUBLIC FORUM DEBATE IS

Public Forum debate, often called PF, is a two-person team debate format. Each side has two debaters. One team supports the resolution, and the other team opposes the resolution.

In WSDA tournaments, the official tournament committee announces the resolution before the tournament. Students are expected to research the topic, prepare arguments, collect evidence, and practice responses before competing.

A PF round is not a general discussion. It is a structured debate with fixed speech times, fixed speech order, and specific roles for each speech. Judges should evaluate the round based on what the debaters say during the round, not based on the judge's personal opinion about the topic.

A judge should vote for the team that does the better job proving its side of the resolution through arguments, evidence, responses, weighing, and final comparison.

SECTION 2: PF RESOLUTION

The debate topic is called the resolution. The affirmative side supports the resolution. The negative side opposes the resolution.

WSDA usually releases several PF resolutions each year. A fall resolution is used for first-semester fall events. A winter resolution is used for the Winter Championship. A spring resolution is used for second-semester and summer events. The National Finals may use a separate resolution.

Example resolution:
Resolved: The worldwide dominance of English is more beneficial than harmful to global cultural development.

Chinese version:
英语在全球范围的主导地位对全球文化发展利大于弊。

Judges should understand the resolution before the round starts, but they should not use outside research or personal knowledge to decide the winner. The judge's decision should be based on the debate that actually happened in the room.

SECTION 3: BEFORE THE ROUND STARTS

When tournament pairings are released, debaters can see the opponent, start time, room, and judge for that round.

Before the scheduled start time, teams should go to their assigned competition room. Judges should also arrive on time, confirm both teams are present, and make sure the room is ready.

At the beginning of the round, the judge conducts a coin flip. The two teams choose heads or tails. The team that wins the coin flip gets the first choice.

The coin-flip winner may choose either:
1. which side to debate, or
2. which speaking order to take.

If the coin-flip winner chooses the side, the other team chooses the speaking order. If the coin-flip winner chooses the speaking order, the other team chooses the side.

Important judge reminder: the first-speaking team is not always the affirmative team. Judges must clearly write down both the side and speaking order before the speeches begin.

SECTION 4: SEATING AND SPEAKING EXPECTATIONS

During the round, the affirmative team sits on the judge's left side. The negative team sits on the judge's right side.

During formal speeches, debaters should stand while speaking. During crossfire periods, direct exchange is allowed according to the format.

Judges should not interrupt speeches unless there is a serious procedural issue, safety issue, or tournament rule issue. Judges should normally track time, listen carefully, and take notes.

Judges should not coach debaters during the round. A judge should not tell a debater what argument to answer, what evidence to read, or how to improve while the round is still happening.

SECTION 5: COMPLETE PF SPEECH ORDER AND TIMES

The PF round follows this speech order:

1. First constructive speech by the first speaker of the first-speaking team: 4 minutes.
2. Second constructive speech by the first speaker of the second-speaking team: 4 minutes.
3. First crossfire between the first speakers: 3 minutes.
4. First rebuttal by the second speaker of the first-speaking team: 4 minutes.
5. Second rebuttal by the second speaker of the second-speaking team: 4 minutes.
6. Second crossfire between the second speakers: 3 minutes.
7. First summary by the first speaker of the first-speaking team: 3 minutes.
8. Second summary by the first speaker of the second-speaking team: 3 minutes.
9. Grand crossfire between all debaters: 3 minutes.
10. First final focus by the second speaker of the first-speaking team: 2 minutes.
11. Second final focus by the second speaker of the second-speaking team: 2 minutes.

Each team has a total of 3 minutes of preparation time. Preparation time can be used before that team's own next speech. A team may use preparation time more than once, but the total cannot exceed 3 minutes.

SECTION 6: PREPARATION TIME RULES

Each team has a total of 3 minutes of preparation time.

A team may call preparation time before its own next speech. A team may use preparation time more than once, but the total time used by that team cannot exceed 3 minutes.

When one team calls preparation time, both teams may use the time to prepare. However, only the team that called preparation time has that time deducted from its total.

Judges should track preparation time carefully. Judges should announce how much preparation time remains if asked.

Preparation time is not a speech. Teams should not use preparation time to make arguments to the judge.

SECTION 8: KEY FORMAT REMINDERS FOR JUDGES

The judge should know both the side and the speaking order before the round begins.

The first-speaking team is not always the affirmative team.

The affirmative team sits on the judge's left. The negative team sits on the judge's right.

The judge should keep time or confirm that timing is being handled correctly.

The judge should not coach debaters during the round.

The judge should evaluate the round based on the speeches and arguments made in the room.

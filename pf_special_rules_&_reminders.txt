PF_SPECIAL_RULES_&_REMINDERS
Document type: Judge training material
Format: Public Forum Debate (PF)
Primary users: Debate judges, tabroom staff, judge trainers
Purpose: Explain special PF rules, evidence checks, prep time, power matching, pairing avoidance, result release rules, elimination round judging procedure, ballot expectations, and high-risk judge mistakes.

SECTION 1: DOCUMENT PURPOSE

This document covers special rules and important reminders for judges in Public Forum debate rounds.

The rules in this document are especially important because mistakes can create tournament administration problems, complaints from teams or parents, delays in tabulation, or unfairness between competitors.

Judges should read this document before judging and should use it as a quick reference when they are unsure about procedure.

SECTION 2: DEBATERS AND SPEAKING POSITIONS

Each PF team has 2 debaters.

Each team may decide its own speaking positions. The team decides which debater will be the first speaker and which debater will be the second speaker.

The first speaker is the debater from that team who speaks earlier in the team’s speech order. The second speaker is the debater from that team who speaks later in the team’s speech order.

Judges should record the correct speaker names and speaking positions on the ballot. Incorrectly recording speaker names, speaker order, side, or team identity can create serious tabroom problems.

Judges should be especially careful after the coin flip because the first-speaking team is not always the affirmative team.

SECTION 3: OVERTIME RULE

Debaters must stop speaking immediately when their speech time ends.

Debaters are not allowed to continue speaking after time has expired.

Judges should give clear time signals if the tournament expects judges to time the round. If a separate timer is used, the judge should still pay attention to whether the speech ends on time.

If a debater continues speaking after time, the judge should not give weight to new arguments or new extensions made after the time limit.

Judges should apply the overtime rule consistently to both teams.

SECTION 4: INTERNET AND MATERIAL LOOKUP DURING THE ROUND

During the round, debaters may use electronic devices to look up internet materials.

However, debaters may not receive help from anyone other than their teammate.

Debaters may not message coaches, parents, teammates outside the round, other students, or any outside person for help during the round.

Debaters may not receive outside strategic advice, arguments, evidence, translations, or responses during the round.

Judges should not actively inspect debaters’ devices unless tournament staff instructs them to do so. If the judge suspects outside assistance, the judge should contact tournament staff or tabroom instead of handling the issue alone.

SECTION 5: PREPARATION TIME RULES

Each team has the right to request preparation time, also called prep time.

Prep time allows debaters to discuss strategy with their teammate during the round.

Each team has a total of 3 minutes of prep time.

A team may request prep time multiple times, but the total prep time used by that team cannot exceed 3 minutes.

A team must ask the judge before using prep time.

A team may only begin prep time after the current speaker has finished speaking. A team may not interrupt a speech to request prep time.

When one team uses prep time, both teams may quietly prepare. However, only the team that requested prep time has the time deducted from its total.

During prep time, debaters may not leave the room.

Judges should track prep time carefully and should be able to tell teams how much prep time remains.

A team could either ask for a fixed amount of prep time (e.g., 20 seconds, 1 minute, ...) or could ask the judge to run the prep time, which means the prep time for the particular team will keep running until the team tells the judge to stop.

SECTION 6: EVIDENCE CHECK RULE

A debater who cites evidence must be able to provide the original text and accurate source of that evidence.

The evidence text should be available in English.

If the evidence comes from an internet source, the debater must provide the original link, and the link must be accessible.

A team may request to check the opposing team’s evidence after any speech has ended.

A team should not interrupt an ongoing speech to request an evidence check.

If Team A asks to check Team B’s evidence, Team B has 1 minute of free time to find and provide the requested evidence. This free time does not count as either team’s prep time.

If Team B cannot provide the evidence within 1 minute, Team B may choose to use its own prep time to continue searching.

If Team B still cannot provide the evidence, Team B may stop searching and accept the consequence of being unable to provide complete evidence.

After Team B provides the evidence to Team A, Team A’s prep time begins if Team A continues checking the evidence.

During evidence checking, participants in the room should only review the evidence. They should not use the evidence check period to prepare new arguments, discuss strategy, or communicate about unrelated issues.

After Team B gives the evidence to Team A, Team B should not take additional actions while Team A reviews the evidence.

Judges should monitor the procedure carefully and keep track of free time and prep time.

SECTION 7: HOW EVIDENCE ISSUES SHOULD AFFECT THE BALLOT

Any questions, challenges, or arguments about evidence must be made as part of a speech.

Judges should not evaluate an evidence challenge as a voting issue unless the debaters explain it in a speech.

Simply failing to find evidence does not automatically prove that the argument is false.

If a team cannot provide evidence, the judge may consider that issue when evaluating the reliability of the argument, but only in the context of how the issue is discussed in the round.

Judges should not independently investigate evidence after the round unless tournament rules or staff specifically instruct them to do so.

Judges should not use their own internet research to decide whether evidence is true or false.

SECTION 8: FEEDBACK AFTER PRE-ELIMINATION ROUNDS

Judges should not give oral feedback after a pre-elimination round.

Judges should not discuss the decision with debaters after a pre-elimination round.

Judges should not explain who won, who lost, or why immediately after a pre-elimination round.

For pre-elimination rounds, releasing the result immediately after the round is strictly prohibited.

The judge should submit the ballot through the required tournament system and allow the tabroom or tournament administration to release results according to official procedure.

This rule prevents information leaks, protects tournament fairness, and avoids confusion before tabulation is complete.

SECTION 9: FEEDBACK AND RESULT RELEASE IN ELIMINATION ROUNDS

In elimination rounds, judges should give the result and feedback after each round if the tournament procedure requires or allows oral disclosure.

Elimination rounds usually require a result to be known immediately because one team advances and the other team is eliminated.

Judges should still give feedback professionally and briefly. Feedback should explain the decision without attacking or embarrassing students in an unfair way.

Feedback should focus on the decisive arguments, weighing, responses, and final voting issues.

Judges should not use feedback to introduce personal opinions about the topic or to criticize students in a disrespectful way.

SECTION 10: THREE-JUDGE PANELS IN ELIMINATION ROUNDS

In elimination rounds, each room may have 3 judges.

Each judge must vote independently.

A judge should not decide based on what another judge thinks.

A judge should not change their decision just because another judge voted differently.

Judges may sit in the same room, but each judge is responsible for their own ballot and their own decision.

Only after all three judges have made their decisions can the result be released.

The result should not be announced before every judge has completed their decision.


SECTION 11: PAIRING AND CONFLICT AVOIDANCE RULES

Tournament pairings should avoid conflicts when possible.

The following avoidance rules may be applied in priority order:

First, a judge should avoid judging a team from the same school or same institution.

Second, a judge who has already judged a team in preliminary rounds should avoid judging the same team again if other judge assignments can reasonably be adjusted. In elimination rounds, this rule applies within the elimination stage and does not necessarily consider preliminary-round repeats.

Third, teams that have already debated each other should avoid debating again during preliminary rounds if possible. This rule usually does not apply in elimination rounds.

Fourth, teams from the same school should avoid debating each other if possible. This rule may be impossible to fully apply if one school has many teams. This rule usually does not apply in elimination rounds.

Judges should report conflicts of interest to tournament staff as soon as possible.

Judges should not wait until the round is over to reveal a conflict.

SECTION 12: AI USE AND OUTSIDE ASSISTANCE

Debaters may use electronic devices during the round to search for evidence or verify source materials online.

Searching for evidence online is allowed. Looking up the original source, checking a link, finding the full text of cited evidence, or locating factual materials may be permitted under the tournament’s evidence and internet lookup rules.

However, debaters may not receive outside assistance during the round. This includes help from coaches, parents, teammates outside the round, other students, online messaging, or AI tools that generate arguments, responses, speech text, crossfire questions, weighing analysis, or strategic advice.

If a judge reasonably believes that a debater is using external help or AI assistance to prepare or deliver speech content during the round, the judge should report the concern to the tabroom or tournament staff.

The judge should not stop the round, accuse the debater directly, inspect private messages, or make a final misconduct ruling alone unless tournament staff instructs the judge to do so.

The judge should record what they observed as specifically as possible. Useful details may include when the concern occurred, what behavior was observed, whether the debater appeared to be receiving generated speech content or strategic advice, and whether the conduct seemed different from ordinary evidence lookup.

Judges should distinguish between allowed evidence searching and prohibited outside assistance. Evidence searching is used to find or verify sources. Prohibited assistance is help that creates, improves, or supplies debate content for the debater during the round.

A judge should continue evaluating the round based on the arguments made unless tournament staff gives a different instruction.

SECTION 13: BALLOT DETAIL REQUIREMENTS

Ballots should include enough detail to show what happened in the round and why the judge voted the way they did.

A ballot should not be too short, too vague, or too general.

A ballot should not simply say “Team A was better,” “better evidence,” “better speaking,” or “more persuasive” without explaining what that means.

A strong ballot identifies the decisive voting issue or issues.

A strong ballot explains how the winning team won those issues.

A strong ballot explains why the losing team’s responses were insufficient, outweighed, dropped, or less important.

A strong ballot connects the decision to arguments actually made in the round.

Judges do not need to summarize every speech. However, judges should provide enough detail for students and tabroom staff to understand the basis of the decision.

SECTION 14: SIDE AND TEAM RECORDING ACCURACY

Judges must carefully check that they record the correct winning team, side, speaker names, speaker points, and ranks if ranks are required.

Recording the wrong side is a serious mistake.

For example, if the judge means to vote for the affirmative team but accidentally selects the negative team, this can create major tabroom problems.

Incorrect side or team recording may cause severe complaints from teams, coaches, tabroom staff, and parents.

Judges should check the ballot before submitting.

Judges should confirm:
1. Which team was affirmative.
2. Which team was negative.
3. Which team spoke first.
4. Which team actually won the judge’s ballot.
5. Whether the selected winner matches the written reason for decision.
6. Whether speaker points are entered for the correct students.

Judges should not rush ballot submission if there is any uncertainty about side or team identity.

SECTION 15: HIGH-RISK JUDGE MISTAKES

High-risk judge mistakes include:
1. releasing a pre-elimination result immediately after the round;
2. giving oral feedback after a pre-elimination round;
3. recording the wrong winning team;
4. recording the wrong side;
5. entering speaker points for the wrong debater;
6. discussing an elimination decision before all judges have voted;
7. changing a vote based on another judge’s opinion;
8. considering evidence challenges that were never made in a speech;
9. inventing missing arguments for a team;
10. submitting a ballot that is too short to explain the decision.

Judges should treat these mistakes as serious because they can affect tournament fairness and create administrative disputes.

SECTION 16: QUICK REFERENCE FOR SPECIAL RULES

Each PF team has 2 debaters.

Each team decides its own first speaker and second speaker.

Debaters must stop speaking when time expires.

Debaters may use electronic devices to look up internet materials during the round.

Debaters may not receive outside help from anyone except their teammate.

Each team has 3 minutes of prep time.

Prep time may not interrupt an ongoing speech.

During prep time, debaters may not leave the room.

Evidence must have original text and accurate source information.

Internet evidence must include an accessible original link.

Evidence checks may be requested after a speech ends.

The team providing evidence has 1 minute of free time to find it.

After the evidence is provided, the checking team’s prep time starts if it continues reviewing.

Evidence issues must be argued in speeches to matter on the ballot.

Failure to find evidence does not automatically prove the argument is false.

Pre-elimination results must not be released immediately after the round.

Oral feedback after pre-elimination rounds is not allowed.

In elimination rounds, judges may give result and feedback according to tournament procedure.

In three-judge elimination panels, each judge votes independently.

Results in elimination rounds should be released only after all judges have made decisions.

Ballots must be detailed enough to explain the decision.

Judges must check side, team, speaker, and winner information carefully before submitting.

PF_VOTING_AND_SPEAKER_SCORING_GUIDE
Document type: Judge training material
Format: Public Forum Debate (PF)
Primary users: New and intermediate debate judges
Purpose: Explain how judges decide the winner, how speaker points should be assigned, and what evaluation categories should be considered.

SECTION 1: JUDGE'S CORE VOTING RESPONSIBILITY

In Public Forum debate, the judge decides the winner based on the debate that happened in the round.

The judge should evaluate both teams' arguments, evidence, impact explanation, persuasiveness, reliability, responses, and final comparison.

The judge should not vote based on personal agreement with the resolution. The judge should not vote based on outside knowledge. The judge should not vote based on which team seemed more likable unless the ballot specifically asks about speaking style as part of speaker points.

The winner should be the team that gives the stronger supported and explained reason to prefer its side of the resolution.

SECTION 2: WHAT DETERMINES THE WINNER

A judge should decide the winner by comparing the two teams' final positions.

The judge should ask:
1. Which arguments are still active by the end of the round?
2. Which arguments were answered, dropped, or successfully defended?
3. Which impacts were better explained?
4. Which team gave clearer weighing or comparison?
5. Which team gave the judge a stronger reason to vote for its side?

The judge should not simply count the number of arguments. One well-explained and well-weighed argument can matter more than several weak arguments.

The judge should not automatically vote for the team with more evidence. Evidence matters only when the team explains what the evidence proves and why it matters.

SECTION 3: ARGUMENTS, IMPACTS, PERSUASIVENESS, AND RELIABILITY

PF decisions should consider arguments, impacts, persuasiveness, and reliability together.

An argument is strong when it has a clear claim, a logical warrant, relevant evidence, and a meaningful impact.

Impact means the reason the argument matters. A team should explain the size, likelihood, timeframe, or importance of its impact when comparing against the opponent.

Persuasiveness means how clearly and effectively the team explains why its side should win. Persuasiveness should be based on debate quality, not just speaking confidence.

Reliability means the judge can trust the argument because it is supported, explained, responsive, and not contradicted by the team's later speeches.

SECTION 4: VOTING MUST MATCH THE ROUND

The judge's vote should be based on the arguments made in the round.

Judges should not invent missing warrants, missing evidence, or missing impact comparison for a team.

Judges should not punish a team for failing to answer an argument that the opposing team never clearly made.

Judges should not use personal knowledge to decide that an argument is true or false if the debaters did not explain it.

If both teams are unclear, the judge should still decide which team did the better debating based on the flow and the final comparison. PF does not allow a tied ballot.

SECTION 5: SPEAKER POINT RANGE

PF speaker points are individual scores assigned to each debater. The score range is 18 to 30 points.

A score of 25 to 26 should be treated as average. This means the debater was understandable and completed the basic role of the speech, but did not show especially strong analysis, evidence use, strategic control, or delivery.

Scores above 26 should indicate performance stronger than average. Scores below 25 should indicate performance weaker than average.

Speaker points should evaluate the individual debater's performance. They should not simply copy the team result.

SECTION 6: WIN-LOSS CONSISTENCY WITH SCORES

The teams' total or comparative speaker scores should be consistent with the judge's win-loss decision if the tournament requires this rule.

The winning team should not receive lower total speaker points than the losing team if the ballot system requires score-win consistency.

Equal individual speaker scores may be allowed. However, the judge cannot submit a tied decision. There must be one winning team and one losing team.

Judges should check the ballot before submitting to make sure the selected winner and the speaker point pattern are consistent with tournament rules.

SECTION 7: SCORING CATEGORY 1 - ANALYSIS

Analysis asks whether the debater explains the most central issues of the resolution and the round.

Strong analysis identifies the key problem, explains why it matters, and connects the argument to the resolution.

Weak analysis may sound like a list of claims without explanation. It may mention important ideas but fail to explain how they prove the team's side.

When scoring analysis, judges should ask:
1. Did the debater explain the core issue?
2. Did the debater connect claims to the resolution?
3. Did the debater explain why the argument matters?
4. Did the debater compare the issue against the opponent's arguments?

SECTION 8: SCORING CATEGORY 2 - EVIDENCE

Evidence asks whether the debater uses fact-based support for arguments.

Strong evidence is relevant, clearly explained, and connected to the claim. The debater should explain what the evidence proves.

Weak evidence may be vague, unrelated, outdated, unexplained, or used as a substitute for reasoning.

Judges should not reward evidence just because it sounds technical. The debater must explain how the evidence supports the argument.

When scoring evidence, judges should ask:
1. Did the debater use factual support?
2. Was the evidence relevant to the claim?
3. Did the debater explain the evidence?
4. Was the evidence connected to the impact or voting issue?

SECTION 9: SCORING CATEGORY 3 - REASONING

Reasoning asks whether the debater uses valid logic to support the argument.

Strong reasoning explains the link between the claim, evidence, and impact. It helps the judge understand why the argument is true and why it matters.

Weak reasoning may rely on assertion. An assertion is a statement made without enough explanation.

Judges should reward debaters who explain the steps of their logic clearly. Judges should be cautious when a debater jumps from a claim to an impact without explaining the connection.

When scoring reasoning, judges should ask:
1. Did the debater explain why the claim is true?
2. Did the debater connect evidence to the claim?
3. Did the debater explain the link to the impact?
4. Did the reasoning answer the opponent's objections?

SECTION 10: SCORING CATEGORY 4 - CROSSFIRE

Crossfire asks whether the debater's questions and answers are clear, concise, civil, and useful.

Strong crossfire clarifies arguments, tests evidence, exposes contradictions, or sets up later responses.

Weak crossfire may be rude, confusing, repetitive, too long, or unrelated to the main issues.

Judges should not treat crossfire as a separate speech, but crossfire can affect speaker points because it shows a debater's understanding, control, and professionalism.

When scoring crossfire, judges should ask:
1. Were the debater's questions clear?
2. Were the answers responsive?
3. Was the debater concise?
4. Was the debater respectful and civil?
5. Did the crossfire help clarify important issues?

SECTION 11: SCORING CATEGORY 5 - REFUTATION

Refutation asks whether the debater effectively answers the opponent's arguments.

Strong refutation directly identifies the opponent's claim and explains why it is wrong, weaker, unsupported, outweighed, or not relevant.

Weak refutation may ignore important arguments, use vague labels, or attack a version of the argument the opponent did not make.

A debater does not need to answer every minor point with equal detail. However, the debater should answer the important arguments that could affect the decision.

When scoring refutation, judges should ask:
1. Did the debater answer the opponent's main arguments?
2. Were the answers specific?
3. Did the debater explain the response?
4. Did the debater avoid leaving decisive arguments unanswered?

SECTION 12: SCORING CATEGORY 6 - SPEAKING

Speaking asks whether the debater's expression is clear, accurate, efficient, and understandable.

Strong speaking is clear, organized, controlled, and easy to follow. The debater uses time efficiently and does not waste time with unnecessary repetition.

Weak speaking may be unclear, disorganized, inaccurate, too fast to understand, too slow to finish important material, or inefficient.

Judges should not reward style over substance. Good speaking helps the argument, but a confident delivery cannot replace missing analysis or weak reasoning.

When scoring speaking, judges should ask:
1. Was the debater understandable?
2. Was the speech organized?
3. Was the delivery efficient?
4. Did the debater use time well?
5. Did the speaking style help the judge follow the argument?

SECTION 13: PRACTICAL SPEAKER POINT BENCHMARKS

18 to 20 points: Very weak performance. The debater may be very difficult to understand, mostly unprepared, or unable to complete the role of the speech.

21 to 24 points: Below average performance. The debater may understand some material but has major problems in clarity, structure, evidence use, refutation, or explanation.

25 to 26 points: Average performance. The debater is generally understandable and completes the basic speech role, but the performance is not especially strong.

27 to 28 points: Strong performance. The debater is clear, prepared, responsive, and strategic. Arguments are explained and connected to the round.

29 to 30 points: Excellent performance. The debater shows exceptional clarity, analysis, evidence use, refutation, weighing, and control of the round.

Judges should avoid giving extremely high or extremely low scores without a clear reason.

SECTION 14: COMMON VOTING MISTAKES TO AVOID

Do not vote based only on speaking confidence.

Do not vote based only on which side had more evidence.

Do not vote based on personal agreement with the topic.

Do not vote on arguments that were not made in the round.

Do not invent missing responses for a team.

Do not ignore the final speeches.

Do not treat crossfire-only comments as decisive unless they are extended and explained in speeches.

Do not give a tied decision. PF requires one winning team.

SECTION 15: BALLOT EXPLANATION

After deciding the winner, the judge should write a reason for decision.

The reason for decision should explain why one team won over the other. It should identify the main voting issue or issues and connect them to the arguments in the round.

A good reason for decision may say:
I voted for the affirmative because their education access impact was extended through summary and final focus, while the negative response about implementation costs was answered in rebuttal and not clearly rebuilt later.

A weak reason for decision may say:
Affirmative was better.

The judge should be specific enough that both teams understand what decided the round.

SECTION 16: QUICK REFERENCE FOR VOTING AND SCORING

PF winner is decided by comparing the teams' arguments, impacts, persuasiveness, reliability, responses, and final weighing.

Speaker points are individual scores from 18 to 30.

A score of 25 to 26 is average.

Speaker point categories include analysis, evidence, reasoning, crossfire, refutation, and speaking.

Scores should be consistent with the ballot rules and win-loss decision.

PF does not allow a tied decision.

Judges should explain the decision clearly and connect it to the round.
